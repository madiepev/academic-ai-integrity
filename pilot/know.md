## Overview of relevant AI tools

Understanding the landscape of AI tools is essential for making informed decisions about when and how to use them in academic work.

### Common AI tools in academic settings

**Large Language Models (LLMs)**
- ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini
- Used for: brainstorming, drafting, summarizing, explaining concepts
- Key limitation: Can generate plausible but incorrect information

**Research Assistants**
- Perplexity, Elicit, Semantic Scholar
- Used for: literature searches, summarizing papers
- Key limitation: May miss nuanced interpretations or recent studies

**Writing Assistants**
- Grammarly, QuillBot, Wordtune
- Used for: grammar checking, paraphrasing, style improvement
- Key limitation: May alter intended meaning or voice

**Code Assistants**
- GitHub Copilot, Tabnine
- Used for: code completion, debugging suggestions
- Key limitation: May suggest inefficient or insecure code

### Key concept: AI hallucinations

**What is a hallucination?** When an AI generates information that sounds confident and plausible but is factually incorrect or entirely fabricated.

**Why do hallucinations occur?**
- AI models generate text based on patterns, not factual databases
- They prioritize fluency and coherence over accuracy
- They cannot verify information in real-time
- They may "fill in gaps" with plausible-sounding but false information

As an experienced AI user, you're ready for a more complex challenge. Below is a realistic academic scenario where multiple issues compound to create problematic AI usage.

### The Scenario

**Context:** A graduate student is writing a literature review on the psychological effects of social media use among teenagers. They're using an AI assistant to help speed up the research process.

**The Conversation:**

<div style="background-color: #f9f9f9; padding: 20px; border-radius: 8px; margin: 20px 0; border-left: 4px solid #0066cc;">

**Student:** "Summarize the key findings from Professor Chen's 2022 meta-analysis on social media and teen mental health."

**AI:** "Professor Chen's 2022 meta-analysis examined 47 studies involving over 15,000 teenagers across 12 countries. The key findings were:
1. Heavy social media use (>3 hours/day) was associated with a 35% increase in depression symptoms
2. The effect was more pronounced in girls (42%) than boys (28%)
3. Instagram and TikTok showed stronger correlations than Facebook or Twitter
4. The study found that social comparison mechanisms explained 68% of the variance in outcomes
Chen concluded that limiting social media use to under 2 hours per day could significantly reduce mental health risks in adolescent populations."

**Student:** "Great! Can you also cite three supporting studies that Chen referenced?"

**AI:** "Certainly! Here are three key studies Chen cited:
1. Martinez, R. & Johnson, L. (2021). 'Social Media Exposure and Adolescent Well-being: A Longitudinal Study.' Journal of Adolescent Psychology, 34(2), 145-162.
2. Williams, K. et al. (2020). 'Instagram Use and Body Image Concerns in Teen Girls.' Digital Health Research, 8(4), 234-251.
3. Thompson, P. & Zhang, H. (2021). 'Comparative Effects of Social Media Platforms on Youth Mental Health.' Developmental Psychology Quarterly, 19(3), 89-107."

**Student:** "Perfect. I'll add these to my literature review."

## Key takeaways for advanced users

- **Hallucinations compound**: Small inaccuracies can cascade into major misrepresentations
- **Specificity invites fabrication**: The more specific your query about things the AI doesn't have access to, the more likely it will hallucinate details
- **Citation verification is non-negotiable**: Every. Single. Reference. Must. Be. Verified.
- **AI is a starting point, never an endpoint**: Use it to guide your research direction, not replace actual research
- **Prompt engineering isn't enough**: Even perfect prompts can't prevent hallucinations when the AI lacks source materials
