Example of an improved version

Research consistently shows that sleep plays a critical role in memory consolidation. During sleep, the hippocampus replays encoded experiences and transfers them to the neocortex for long-term storage (Stickgold, 2005, Nature Reviews Neuroscience). Sleep deprivation disrupts this process, with studies linking it to impaired declarative memory performance (Killgore, 2010, Progress in Brain Research).

However, the relationship between sleep deprivation and exam performance is more nuanced than it may appear. Effects depend on the type of memory task, the duration of deprivation, and individual differences. Walker's (2017) popular account in Why We Sleep has been criticised in the academic literature for overstating the permanence and severity of sleep deprivation effects (Frankild et al., 2020). Some evidence also suggests that strategic massed studying can benefit certain types of recognition memory in the short term.

For a study advice essay, the evidence suggests that prioritising sleep is generally beneficial for retention — but this advice is most applicable to extended periods of study, not as a universal rule that overrides all other considerations.
